Leaving the Berth · SYSAC Solo

  1. Engine. Warm 5 min in neutral. Check raw water cooling flow, VHF Ch 16, AIS on. Lifejacket clipped to jackstay before you leave the cockpit.
  2. Assess. Wind and tide — tide usually dominates at SYSAC. Plan to spring off against the dominant force.
  3. Single up. Midships spring, both ends on your cleats. Dab forward against it: stern walks out. Astern: bow walks out.
  4. Slip. Walk to bow, slip spring, step back aboard — no shore help needed. Slow ahead, rudder amidships.
  5. Wind pinning you on. Hold spring + engine 30 s longer than feels needed. Let the stern walk fully off before slipping.
  6. Clear. Tidy lines and fenders in open water, not in the channel.

Tawe Lock · Solo Checklist

Solo Hoist · Main (Slab Reefing)

  1. Motor head-to-wind in open water past the breakwater. Engage tiller pilot or lash helm.
  2. Topping lift snug. Main halyard clear, no overrides.
  3. Hoist, tensioning the halyard fully — luff should be bar-tight with no scallops.
  4. Sheet in, set kicker. Bear away, engine off, settle on course.

Slab Reefing (jiffy reef)

  1. Ease mainsheet and topping lift slightly to take load off the luff. Ease halyard until the luff cringle reaches the reefing hook (or clip the tack reefing line).
  2. Haul the clew reefing line until the foot is bar-tight along the boom. Cleat it off firmly.
  3. Re-tension the halyard fully. Tidy the bunt with the reef points — loosely, they are not load-bearing.
  4. Kicker stays on throughout. Can be done solo in under 2 minutes once practised.
  5. Shake the reef out in reverse: ease clew line first, hoist halyard, then release tack.

Genoa Solo · 150%

  1. Both sheets led to cockpit winches before leaving the dock. Furling line clear on its clutch.
  2. Bear off to a beam reach to unfurl — releases load on the furling drum.
  3. Sheet from leeward winch, tail with self-tailer. Crank with handle locked in cleated position so both hands stay on it.
  4. To reef: furl while bearing away slightly. Move genoa car aft one hole per ~10–15% furled — foot shortens, sheeting angle must follow.
  5. Above 18 kn TWS: furl at least 20–30% before it gets lively.

Heaving-To · Solo Superpower

Use to reef, navigate, eat, or wait for the lock. Ava heaves-to beautifully up to F6.

  1. From close-hauled on starboard, tack without releasing the genoa sheet. Genoa backs.
  2. Push tiller to leeward and lash it.
  3. Ease mainsheet until the main is just drawing or luffing softly.
  4. Ava sits 50–60° off the wind, ~1 kn forward + 1 kn leeway. The slick to windward calms the sea. Hands-off for 10+ minutes.

Anchoring · Solo

Rule of thumb for scope: 5:1 in calm, 7:1 in wind or swell, 10:1 in heavy weather. Scope = total chain + warp paid out ÷ depth at high water.

Depth at anchor (m)Scope 5:1Scope 7:1Minimum chain
2 m10 m out14 m out10 m chain, then warp
3 m15 m out21 m out15 m chain
5 m25 m out35 m out25 m chain
8 m40 m out56 m out40 m chain, then warp
10 m50 m out70 m out40 m chain + 30 m warp
15 m75 m out105 m out40 m chain + 65 m warp

Always use depth at high water when calculating scope — if you anchor at LW in a 9 m tidal range, the depth can be 9 m greater at HW. In the Bristol Channel this matters enormously.

Solo Procedure

  1. Approach. Head slowly upwind or up-tide (whichever is stronger). Come to almost zero speed over the ground, not just through the water.
  2. Drop. Let go when the boat is just starting to make sternway. Don't throw — just release and let the chain run freely.
  3. Pay out scope. Let chain run as Ava drifts back. Control the rate with the windlass brake — never let the chain pile up on the anchor.
  4. Set. Once scope is out, snub the chain firmly. Put the engine astern at slow to medium revs for 30 seconds. Watch a transit — if fixed, the anchor has set.
  5. Shackle a snubber (length of rope/chain hook) to take the shock load off the windlass gipsy.
  6. Anchor ball / light. Day shape in the bow for daytime; anchor light (all-round white) at night. Required by COLREGS.
  7. Drag watch. Note two transits at right angles. Check every 20–30 min. On the Pi, OpenCPN's anchor watch alarm will alert if the boat moves more than your set radius.

Bristol Channel note: With 9–10 m spring tides, anchor where you won't be aground at LW. Check the charted depth and the tide state. In Oxwich Bay, Three Cliffs, or Pwll Du — all drying in patches — do this carefully.

MOB · Solo

If you go over solo without a tether, you will not get back aboard. Wear it. Clip on. Always.

  • Lifejacket with crotch strap and integrated AIS PLB
  • Tether to jackstay before leaving the cockpit — short (1 m) to mast, long (2 m) along deck
  • Engine kill cord on wrist when motoring
  • Shore contact before departure: ETA, route, vessel name, call-time

What is Celestial Navigation?

Celestial navigation finds your position using the angles between celestial bodies (sun, moon, stars, planets) and the horizon, measured with a sextant. It requires no electronics and works anywhere on earth. For RYA Yachtmaster Offshore it is a syllabus requirement — and it is a genuine skill worth having as a backup to GPS on a passage like Swansea → Scilly.

Start with the noon latitude sight — the simplest observation in celestial nav, requiring only a sextant and a watch. It gives you your latitude directly, with no sight reduction tables needed.

Equipment You Need

SextantAny bubble or optical sextant — even a plastic Davis Mk 15 is fine to learn on
Accurate watchGPS time or radio-corrected — error of 1 second = 0.25 nm position error
Nautical AlmanacCurrent year — gives GHA and Dec of all bodies
Sight reduction tablesHO 249 or AP3270 — or use the calculator on this page
Chart / plotting sheetTo plot your lines of position
Pencil, parallel rules, dividersStandard nav tools

Key Terms

HoObserved altitude — Ha corrected for refraction (and limb if sun/moon)

DecDeclination — celestial equivalent of latitude; from the Almanac
LHALocal Hour Angle — GHA minus your longitude (W)
InterceptHo − Hc in minutes of arc = nautical miles towards/away from body

The Noon Sight · Latitude by Meridian Passage

At local noon the sun crosses your meridian (due south in the northern hemisphere) and reaches its highest point in the sky. At that exact moment, a single sextant reading gives you your latitude directly — no time needed, no sight reduction tables.

Procedure
  1. Find local noon. From the Almanac, get today's GHA of the sun at 1200 UTC. Your longitude in time (divide degrees by 15 to get hours) subtracted from 1200 gives local apparent noon (LAN) — roughly 1200 + 20 min for Swansea (~4°W).
  2. Track the sun up. Start watching ~15 min before LAN. As the sun climbs, keep it on the horizon. It will slow, stop — that is meridian passage. Read off the maximum altitude (Hs).
  3. Apply corrections to get Ho (see calculator below).
  4. Calculate zenith distance (ZD). ZD = 90° − Ho. This is how far you are from the point directly below the sun.
  5. Get latitude. Latitude = Declination ± ZD.
    — If you are north of the sun's declination: Lat = Dec + ZD
    — If you are south of the sun's declination: Lat = Dec − ZD

At Swansea in summer, the sun's Dec is ~+20° to +23°N. Your latitude is ~51.6°N. The sun transits to the south of you, so ZD = Lat − Dec. Example: Ho = 62°, Dec = +21°N → ZD = 28°, Lat = 21 + 28 = 49°N... until you correct for your real altitude. The calculator does this for you.

Sun Sight · Line of Position (LOP)

A time sight taken when the sun is not on the meridian gives a line of position — you are somewhere along that line. Two sights, or a morning sight + noon sight, give a fix.

Taking the Sight
  1. Note the exact GMT time to the second when you take the sight — this is critical. 1 second of error = 0.25 nm.
  2. Measure Hs and apply corrections (IE, dip, refraction, semi-diameter) to get Ho.
  3. From the Almanac, get GHA and Dec of the sun for that exact UTC time (interpolate for the minutes and seconds using the Increments and Corrections tables).
  4. Calculate LHA = GHA + East longitude (or GHA − West longitude). Use your assumed position (AP) — nearest whole degree of latitude and longitude that makes LHA a whole number.
  5. Enter HO 249 or AP3270 with your AP latitude, declination, and LHA to get Hc and Zn.
  6. Intercept = Ho − Hc (in minutes). Positive = towards the body (Towards); negative = away.
  7. Plot: from your AP, plot Zn. Mark intercept distance along it. Draw the LOP at 90° to the azimuth line through that point.
The Running Fix

Two observations of the same body at different times give a running fix — the standard technique on a passage.

  1. Morning sight (~0900–1000) — sun in the SE, bearing about 120°–140°. Plot LOP₁.
  2. Advance LOP₁ along your course and speed to the time of the second sight.
  3. Noon sight — take a meridian altitude. This gives your latitude directly (a horizontal LOP).
  4. Where the advanced morning LOP crosses the noon latitude line = your noon position.
  5. Afternoon sight (~1500) — sun in the SW. Cross with advanced noon latitude for a 3-LOP fix.

The morning/noon/afternoon three-LOP pattern is the classic ocean passage day. Repeat each day.

Star & Planet Sights

Stars and planets are observed at dawn and dusk during civil twilight — when the horizon is still visible but the sky is dark enough to see the body. The brief window is typically 20–30 minutes.

Best stars from UK waters
PolarisDue north — altitude ≈ your latitude. Simplest star sight.
ArcturusBright orange, high in summer sky. Easy to identify.
VegaVery bright, nearly overhead in summer from 51°N.
CapellaBright, NE sky, year-round from UK.
SiriusBrightest star in the sky — winter/spring from UK.
Polaris latitude sight

Polaris sits almost exactly above the North Pole. Its altitude above the horizon equals your latitude — within about 1°. A small correction (the p correction from the Almanac) refines it to within 1 mile.

  1. Observe Polaris at twilight. Note GMT time.
  2. Apply IE, dip, refraction to get Ho.
  3. From Almanac: get LHA Aries for your UTC time and approximate longitude.
  4. Enter Polaris tables (inside back cover of Almanac) to get the a₀, a₁, a₂ corrections.
  5. Latitude = Ho − 1° + a₀ + a₁ + a₂

Practice Plan · Getting Started

  1. Week 1 — at home. Read Chapter 1–3 of Celestial Navigation by Tom Cunliffe (the standard RYA text). Get familiar with the Almanac layout.
  2. Week 2 — ashore. Practice noon sights on land with a known position. Compare your result to GPS. Aim for within 5 miles to start.
  3. Week 3 — on Ava. Take a noon sight on every passage. Cross-check with GPS — don't rely on it yet, just observe the error.
  4. Week 4. Add morning sights. Plot running fixes. Aim for GPS cross-checks within 10 miles.
  5. Yachtmaster level. Three-body star fix at dawn, accurate to 5 miles. One night passage where cel nav is your primary method.
Common mistakes
Watch errorAlways record watch error at sight time — 4 seconds = 1 mile
Wrong limbNote lower or upper limb — confusing them puts you 32′ out
Rocking the sextantSwing the arc gently — the lowest point of the arc is the true altitude
Forgetting dipAlways apply dip — it is always a subtraction
Dec sign errorN and S declination — getting it wrong flips your latitude by 2 × Dec
Assumed positionAP must give whole-degree LHA — check this before entering tables

What to Buy First

  1. Victron SmartShunt 500A (~£85) — fit this immediately. It wires in-line with your negative battery cable and gives you real-time SOC%, voltage, current and estimated run time. One VE.Direct USB cable connects it to the Pi. This is the single most useful instrument you can add.
  2. Victron SmartSolar MPPT 75/15 (~£85) — replace any existing charge controller with this. Connects your Allpowers panels via MC4, charges the battery optimally, and reports solar yield to the Pi via a second VE.Direct cable. The SmartShunt and MPPT talk to each other and show a combined system state in the VictronConnect app on your iPad.
  3. Wema tank senders + MCP3008 ADC (~£80 total) — one sender per tank. The ADC chip is a tiny board that connects to the Pi's SPI GPIO pins and converts the sender's resistance to a percentage. OpenPlotter's plugin maps it to Signal K automatically.
  4. Victron Blue Smart IP22 12/20 charger (~£130) — for shore power at SYSAC. Bluetooth monitoring from the iPad via VictronConnect. No Pi connection needed — it reports directly to the app.
  5. Float switch for bilge (~£10) — wire directly to a Pi GPIO pin. A pull-up resistor and two lines of Python in OpenPlotter gives you a bilge alarm that appears in Signal K.

Total Tier 1+2: ~£300–320. Gives you full battery, solar, and tank monitoring all visible in this app and in the VictronConnect iPad app simultaneously.

Setup on the Pi

After cloning the repo, run the admin server once to test it, then add it to autostart.

1 · Run manually (test it works)
cd ~/ava-nav
python3 ava-admin.py
2 · Auto-start with systemd (runs on every boot)
sudo nano /etc/systemd/system/ava-admin.service

Paste this into the file:

[Unit]
Description=Ava-Nav admin server
After=network.target

[Service]
ExecStart=/usr/bin/python3 /home/pi/ava-nav/ava-admin.py
WorkingDirectory=/home/pi/ava-nav
Restart=on-failure
User=pi

[Install]
WantedBy=multi-user.target
sudo systemctl enable ava-admin
sudo systemctl start ava-admin
